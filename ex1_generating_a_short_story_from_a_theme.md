# Objective: Practice creative writing through prompts.

**Prompt 1:**
```
Write a short story about friendship in under 300 words.
```

**Response 1:**
```
When the power went out across the neighborhood, everyone else panicked. Tolu and Emeka celebrated.

It was the third blackout that week, and the heat pressed against the walls like a stubborn guest. But for Tolu and Emeka, darkness meant adventure. Armed with a small rechargeable lamp and a pack of biscuits, they met outside their houses like explorers planning an expedition.

They had been friends since primary school—since the day Emeka shared his lunch after Tolu forgot hers. Since the day Tolu stood up for Emeka when older boys teased him for stuttering. Their friendship was stitched together with small, brave moments.

That night, they lay on the warm concrete, staring at a sky suddenly crowded with stars. Without the usual hum of generators, the world felt wider.

“What do you think you’ll be?” Emeka asked.

“A pilot,” Tolu said quickly. “I want to see everything from above.”

Emeka was quiet for a while. “I think I’ll build things. Bridges, maybe. So people can cross to better places.”

They laughed at how serious they sounded. Then they began naming the stars after themselves, their teachers, even the biscuit brand.

When the lights flickered back on, the magic didn’t disappear. It simply folded itself into memory.

Years later, when life became louder and harder, they would remember those dark evenings—the stars, the dreams, the shared biscuits. They would remember that friendship is not just about growing up together.

It’s about helping each other grow.
```

**Prompt 2:**
```
Write a short story about personal growth in under 300 words.
```

**Response 2:**
```
When Ada failed her first university exam, she hid the paper at the bottom of her drawer as if it were something contagious.

She had always been “the smart one.” The girl teachers praised. The daughter relatives pointed to. Failure didn’t fit the story she had written about herself.

For days, she avoided her friends and ignored her phone. But silence has a way of growing heavy. One evening, she pulled the paper out and stared at the red marks. Instead of shame, she tried something new: curiosity.

Where had she lost marks? What had she misunderstood?

The answers were uncomfortable. She realized she had relied on last-minute cramming, on talent over discipline. She liked the idea of being smart more than the work of becoming better.

So she changed small things.

She started studying an hour each morning before lectures, even when her bed felt like a magnet. She asked questions in class, even when her voice trembled. She formed a study group and admitted, for the first time, “I don’t understand this.”

Weeks passed. Growth did not arrive like a trumpet blast. It came quietly—through improved quizzes, clearer notes, steadier confidence.

When the next results were released, Ada’s hands still shook. But this time, she smiled.

The grade was good. Not perfect. But earned.

She didn’t rush to hide the paper. She pinned it above her desk—not as proof that she was smart, but as proof that she could change.

And for the first time, she understood: growth isn’t about protecting who you were.

It’s about building who you are becoming.
```

**Prompt 3:**
```
Write a short story about work-life balance in under 300 words.
```

**Response 3:**
```
Kunle measured his days in notifications.

Emails before sunrise. Meetings stacked like dominoes. Deadlines that chased him into the night. His laptop glowed brighter than the moon outside his window.

He told himself it was temporary. “Just this quarter,” he’d say. But quarters kept repeating.

One Saturday afternoon, while answering “just one more message,” he heard laughter outside. His younger sister was teaching their father how to ride her old bicycle in the compound. They wobbled. They fell. They laughed harder.

Kunle watched from behind the curtain, fingers still hovering over his keyboard.

A thought slipped in, quiet but sharp: *When did I last laugh like that?*

He closed the laptop.

Not dramatically. Not forever. Just gently.

He stepped outside, blinking at the sunlight like someone emerging from a cave. His sister waved him over. “Help me hold it steady!”

For the next hour, he forgot about performance reviews and pending reports. He ran behind the bicycle, shouting instructions, laughing when his father nearly steered into a flower pot.

That evening, the emails were still there. The deadlines hadn’t vanished. But something had shifted.

On Monday, Kunle spoke to his manager. He reorganized his schedule. No emails after 8 p.m. Sundays offline. Short walks between meetings. It felt small. Almost insignificant.

But life began to feel wider.

He still worked hard. He still cared. But he no longer measured his worth by exhaustion.

Because success, he realized, wasn’t about filling every minute.

It was about making sure some of them truly belonged to him.
```

**Deduction**

All the responses were stories however, each was tailored to the specific theme contained in the prompt.
